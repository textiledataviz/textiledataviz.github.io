"Creating data" can take a lot of different forms, but crucially, it involves you doing the observation, or the transformation of some other medium (e.g. text, photos, etc.) into data, or gathering things into a data set. You don't have to have created the other medium / material you're transforming yourself, but you should be the person making the decisions about what to include, how to transform it, etc. 

## Personal data

Personal data is fairly commonly used for textile data visualization projects. There are many possible angles, and it aligns well with what you might already be tracking or journaling for other reasons. Here are just a few examples:

#### Reading logs

Reading logs are common across several crafts. For instance, crocheting a [[Granny square]] in colors that match the cover of each book you read, or embroidering a design in different colors reflecting other data about your reading habits (e.g. genre, physical/audiobook/ebook, etc.) When reading in an academic context, "books" may make less sense than pages or words, and type of reading (e.g. academic article, novel, lab manual, popular non-fiction).

#### Feelings / mood

Taxonomies of feelings are notoriously messy, but you can select one to work with and pare it down to the distinctions that seem valuable and relevant for you. The [[Textile Makerspace Guestbook Loom]] is one simplified example, with feelings rendered using pony beads, and intended activity in the space represented with yarn colors.

#### Interactions

It can be meaningful to keep a log of the people you interact with and how over the course of the day, using one of the [[Properties]] to capture things like role, relationship, or part of your life (e.g. family,  coworkers, old friends, partners) and another to capture the type of interaction.

Another approach could be to focus on a single relationship over some period of time. You could keep track of different modes and quantities of communication (e.g. texting, Zoom, phone, in-person), GIFs or memes or photos exchanged, number of times each person laughed or cried (and why), or many other things.

#### Clothes

What colors are you wearing right now? What kind of outfit did you wear yesterday? How often did you wear a hat? Does your footwear change over the course of a day or a week? How often do you wear things you make yourself? Everyone wears clothes, regardless of how much or little thought they put into them, and there are lots of things you could keep track of for a textile data visualization.

Alternately, you could take on a project like Wendy Ward's [[Wearing Tally]] stitches, where she adds a small French knot [[Embroidery]] stitch to every garment (or a small label she's attached to every garment) she owns as she wears it. 

## Creating data from text

Text is a rich source material to adapt into data that you can visualize with textiles. One of the easiest approaches to look at occurrences or frequencies of different terms -- or alternately, the presence or absence of terms across different texts.

To do this, you need a digital, searchable version of the text(s) you're analyzing. Plain text (.txt) files are ideal. If you're not comfortable writing code, you can use a tool like [Voyant](https://voyant-tools.org/) to explore the texts, search for terms and phrases and identify their frequency across the text(s). (See this [Programming Historian tutorial on Voyant](https://programminghistorian.org/en/lessons/corpus-analysis-voyant-tools) for tips on how to get started.)

Examples of woven data from text include [Computers, Archives and Librarians in Star Trek](https://quinndaedal.com/textiles/computers-archives-librarians-star-trek/) and [The Locked Loom 1: Gideon the Ninth](https://quinndaedal.com/blog/2023/12/21/locked-loom-1-gideon-the-ninth/).
