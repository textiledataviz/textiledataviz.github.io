Acquired data is data that you didn't [[Created Data|create yourself]]. You didn't observe it, you didn't transform it, you can't personally speak to those aspects of the data's provenance. Depending on where and how you got it, you might be able to find out some things about the data. For instance, [Data Cards](https://sites.research.google/datacardsplaybook/) are a documentation practice in the data science community that addresses some of the kinds of questions and ambiguities that you would have access to if you had created the data yourself.

## Examples of acquired data

### Temperature data

Starting with the 2016 [[Tempestry Project]], temperature blankets (and other textile-crafted objects) have been the best-known example of data visualization with textiles. Temperature data is well-archived, easily accessible, and seems alluringly straightforward. But especially if you're using daily temperature reports and creating your data set day by day, you might want to think about what value(s) you want to capture. Highs? Lows? Both? The "actual temperature", or one of the other values that takes into consideration other factors and might be more representative of how you experienced it (e.g. humidity, wind chill)? 

What story are you trying to tell with the data? Is it about the temperature in a specific place all year, or your year as experienced through temperature? If you live somewhere with a cold winter, and you take a vacation in the middle of the winter to somewhere tropical, do you keep recording the values from back at home, or the warmer temperatures of your vacation destination, or both?


### Civic data

There's a lot of data available online that has been collected and distributed by civic entities -- at the local, regional, and national level. As different entities take political power, what data is collected and shared can change; take care not to casually combine data sets from different locations or different times without doing the work to ensure they actually represent the same thing. Even labels like "name", "date", and "location" may actually mean different things in different contexts.

### Organizational data

Many organizations gather data and either share it publicly or internally. When using internal data, it's a judgement call about whether a textile data visualization would reveal too much sensitive information; ask around in advance to be sure, some people can react strongly to their data -- even obliquely -- being used in data visualizations, with concerns about what it might say about them. 

[[CIDR-Coeur]] is one example of using internal work data (Slack messages) for a weaving. Our internal work culture is such that there was no particular value judgement -- positive or negative -- associated with being on Slack. Also, all the data is color-coded by job type rather than person, with the exception of our boss, so there is some added layer of obfuscation of particular data points individual colleagues.

### Scientific data

Erica Lord's [[The Codes We Carry]] replicates computer-produced DNA/RNA microarrays from diseases disproportionately affecting Indigenous communities using beadwork. Adapting existing visualizations where the data has gone through a computer-based processing pipeline is one approach; taking data at an earlier stage (e.g. directly from instruments, or after some basic level of processing) and creating a visualization using the conventions of the textile you're working in is another.
