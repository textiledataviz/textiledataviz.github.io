The academic paper "[Conceptualizing legal change as ‘norm-knitting’ through the example of the environmental human right](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/leiden-journal-of-international-law/article/conceptualizing-legal-change-as-normknitting-through-the-example-of-the-environmental-human-right/B71D455D6CC0C09AC0277B29CC4639F0?utm_campaign=shareaholic&utm_medium=copy_link&utm_source=bookmark)" by Dorothea Endres in the *Leiden Journal of International Law*,  2023; 36(4) offers [[Textiles/Knitting/index|knitting]] as an extended metaphor for how international law is made, using the example of environmental law. 

She maps needles to the actors, the pattern to norm types, tension as loose / tight norms, wool as resources, shape as the norm's form, and colors as origins. While this is not, strictly speaking, data, it is a framework where one could model data from specific instances of successful or unsuccessful law-making, and the paper includes images of what the results would look like as these properties change.
